# Coaching Philosophies — in Nathan's own words (voice memo series)

## Stealing third
Stealing third is a signature part of Nathan's teams, built on his own playing career — he was regarded as one of the best base runners in the world, and stealing third was a challenge he mastered.

**The reads before the green light:**
- Watch the shortstop first: depth, range, which side they shade, and how they set into position every pitch — including which foot they land on. That tells you how quickly they can cover third.
- Know the catcher's game plan on the hitter. Throwing outside to a right-hand hitter shades the shortstop further left — but it holds the third baseman back.
- Use a late fake bunt to move the third baseman: bring them forward and off the base just before the steal goes.
- Watch whether the infield is communicating, and what they are saying. Is the shortstop telling the third baseman "if you go in, I've got third"? Is the third baseman calling in or holding back? **If they are not talking to each other at all — red rope, ready to go.** Silence in the infield is the green light.
- The trick (keep it between us, not the umpires): nobody expects a steal of third — especially the umpire. 99 times out of 100 the runner can leave second a touch early and no one is watching for it.

**Expect the chaos and plan to cash it:** coming into third, expect confusion — the third baseman scrambling back, mixing up with the shortstop, throws going through to the outfield. Come in already looking to score: up quickly from the slide (or staying on the feet if possible), reading for the overthrow, and taking home when it comes.

**Who gets the green light:** runners who can make these reads — it is a skill taught and earned, not a raw-speed award. The steal of third is won on observation before the pitch, not pace after it.

**Coaching lesson:** base stealing is a reading game. Teach runners to study the defence's habits — setup feet, shading, communication — and train them to expect the defensive mistake and be ready to take the extra base.

## Baserunning philosophy — good vs fast, and how to train it
Like throwing, hitting and pitching, start with the hardest thing first: the best base runners have speed, first and foremost.

**Speed comes first:** speed training and plyometrics — toe taps and sprinters' drills apply directly to softball, getting the foot up and down as fast as possible. Train top-end speed, not just the 5–10 metre burst: work the 20–30 metre range. Home to home is roughly 11–12 seconds, about 3.5 seconds between bases — so train to run hard for 14–15 seconds when needed.

**Technique on leads and takeoffs:** sprinter mechanics — the crossover and the power out of the blocks. On the first jump, Nathan uses the base itself as leverage to push off. The old school taught standing beside the base; Nathan finds being ON the base gives more height and a longer, better first step and jump.

**What separates a GOOD baserunning team from a fast one:** a good baserunning team doesn't get out. They are smart — moving ahead on the percentage plays, holding when they need to, aggressive when they have to be — and above all safe on the base paths. Caution: "safe" alone can hide laziness or fear. The real gauge: if you are getting thrown out on the bases, you have learning to do; if you are taking extra bases AND getting in safe, you are a good baserunning team.

**Training it — in parts:** practise leads, takeoffs, and every slide: straight slide, hook slide on both legs, dives on both sides, sliding past the bag and coming back, the dive-and-swim, pop-up slides — all learned the safe way. Nathan runs sliding sessions on wet-grass days (rain earlier, clear evening = perfect). Ground care while you do it: don't carve one muddy patch — train in one area, and when the ground starts to tear, move to a fresh spot. Look after the grounds and the grounds people will look after you.

**Running the bases:** know which foot touches the inside of the base, control your body angle around the turns, and learn to scan. The third-base coach gives the signals — but on the 50/50 ball, where the coach can't know what you're thinking, learn to turn your head, take your own look, and make your own decision.

## 3-0 counts — hitting philosophy
Nathan has been on both sides of this across his career. Two approaches exist: swing, or take. The old-school culture was always take — banking the walk if the umpire called a ball. But Nathan evolved on this a long time ago and is now a firm proponent of **swinging 3-0**: if the pitcher is going to lolly one down the middle, stick it out — make sure they never, ever get a free strike on 3-0.

**How it runs in Nathan's teams:**
- It is talked about at training and grained into the team culture before games, not sprung on anyone.
- Players who aren't comfortable swinging 3-0 in their heart can take for the team — and if it goes to a strike, back yourself on 3-2 and come back. Either way is not a negative; learn from the at-bat.
- The recent numbers in Nathan's teams lean strongly toward swinging — the success percentage on 3-0 swings is very high.
- **When you swing 3-0, swing BIG.** Give up nothing. Swing as if it is right down the middle — a total miss is fine. Swing as hard as you can.
- **The signal:** thumbs up or thumbs down from Nathan. Mostly it's a thumbs up — the default mindset is you're going. In tighter game situations the hitter looks to Nathan, who will signal if they're not. Thumbs down happens, but far less than thumbs up.

## Outfield positioning
The centre fielder works in with the shortstop — and with the catcher, that trio runs the defensive alignment together. The centre fielder should know the game plan ahead of time and be working to it without needing the coach.

**Where the coach steps in during the game:** most outfielders play 50/50 — able to cover in, back, left and right. Sometimes the coach has to push those odds one way or the other, especially late in the game or trying to stop runs.

**Specific alignments:**
- **Lefty bunt-slapper:** left fielder comes in to play the gap over shortstop/third base — the "5-6 deep infield, shallow left field" position. Right fielder still holds, centre fielder shades toward that centre-left gap.
- **Playing extremely in:** late game, stopping a run — when a deep fly ball can't throw the winner out at home anyway, play in for everything shallow.

**The standing jobs:** on balls through the gap, outfielders are the eyes and ears for the player picking up. And the outfield job, over and over: **hit the cut. Keep it simple — pick it up, hit the cut. If in doubt, hit the cut.** The biggest thing Nathan wants from his outfield beyond that: throwing runners out, especially in big-game situations.

## Coaching from first base vs third base
Nathan's philosophy: **coach from the side your dugout is on.** The main reason is control of the team — being next to the dugout to have conversations, get runners and fielders ready for the next inning.

**The two boxes:**
- **Third base** is the better side of the diamond to coach from — there's a lot of diamond work, moving around, being available. The third-base coach's big job is guiding the runner from second through to home. Overall it's the most important box because you can directly affect the end of the game.
- **First base** is still valuable: guiding the runner on first across to second — into scoring position. It's also in-game learning: talking to the runner each pitch about the ways to get across the diamond (the ones who don't need it, don't need it). Signals can be given from either side — it's just where the hitter is looking, so the box is largely irrelevant for that.

**The big-game move:** in the biggest tournaments, if Nathan's dugout is first-base side and it's a tight game, he will call timeout and swap with the third-base coach — going to third to help the runner get from third to home and possibly win the game. That's when being the third-base coach matters most.

Nathan trains his coaches and players who fill these boxes — baserunning trainings covering depth reads and the coach's role, so anyone in the box knows the job.

## Scorers, lineup changes, and the rules
Nathan's mother is a national badge-holding scorer — he has been around scoring his whole life, a self-described stat rat from way back who learned young to read a whole game off a score sheet.

**The scorer is integral to any team:**
- They are the guide to the rules — what can and can't be done. There are complexities around the **DP and Flex** that teams should be making more of. The key: using the DP/Flex with a position player allows unlimited pitching changes between the starting pitcher and the DP — the starting pitcher can come off the mound but remain in the game. Nathan saw Texas Tech softball work this through a whole game, and the Ramblers use it too; it all hangs on how the lineup is created. Coaches should study the mechanism with their scorer rather than be handed it — knowing WHY the lineup is built a certain way is the edge.
- A proactive scorer calls the batting order as the team comes off the diamond — who's first up, who's on deck. Simple, but it lets the team ease in and relax instead of forcing the recall; someone reaffirms it every time.
- A scorer travelling with the team can be awkward if they don't quite fit in — management should take them in. They are, always will be, and should be a very important part of the team.
- **Coaches should use scorers far more for stats:** understand how players perform and WHO they perform against — then train to lift the performances against the ones they struggle with, and maintain and protect the strengths they already hold.

## Bunt defence — and the lost art of the bunt
The pure bunt is rarely used these days — a few players use a short game, but the true bunt has faded. That's a mistake: **it's a weapon power hitters with some speed should be using**, because you're not always seeing the ball well.

**The masters Nathan has seen:** the best was Dean Wallace — "Shocker" — who had a really nasty LATE bunt: from his hitting position, with the rise ball running across the plate, he'd drop straight into the bunt late, bang it down, gone — and he wasn't even quick, middle-to-low speed range in an average team. Thomas Enoka has a very good late drop bunt too. Internationally, nobody stands out to Nathan as a great one.

**The defence itself — simple philosophy:**
- If the batter is scouted as a bunter: you're in or you're out. No half measures.
- Trying to get an out: play both corners in — first and third — and they need to be good enough to make the play that comes to them, with the pitcher helping on anything back to the middle. Take your out.
- Playing for the double or triple play: run a game-plan shift — third base in, first base off, the wheel rotating around the clock one way or the other.

## Trials and selections — from the selector's chair
Nathan has run rep trials (including Auckland U19 boys) and takes a season-long, longer-term development view of a player over any single trial performance.

**What he's really looking for at trials — behaviours:**
- People who want to be there. People doing extra. People not afraid to look like a try-hard — because it means they don't care what anyone else thinks; they're getting their own work done. He likes a hustler.
- Players who listen but push boundaries. Too many sit back and never try new things. Nathan wants players who experiment in every part of the game, especially defensively — his teams have run crazy defensive shifts just to learn: what does the hitter think when a big shift goes on? What does it do to their psyche? What do the umpires do, the crowd? How do we feel? Until you do some things, you won't know. Put all the cards on the table, put the work in, see what it looks like, decide from there.
- Highly talented, highly skilled players will likely make it regardless — with them the work is the mental side and making sure they're a good person.

**Running it fair — and the letdown:** part of the coach's job is letting down those who miss out properly, AND reminding those who made it who missed — keeping the selected humble and the missed hopeful. Missing a team is not the end: there are other opportunities, other associations to place players who want rep softball, and that should go through the association.

**Nathan's own selection scar (told to players who miss out):** at 14 he missed the Auckland U17s — a team he'd made the year before — because he hadn't attended their off-season clinics. The trial itself had no bearing. The reason he'd missed the clinics: he was in Auckland rugby at the same time, and he was 14. That same rep year he still made the Auckland U15s, the Auckland U19s, and the Auckland Men's Fastpitch team for an exhibition against Japan — where, young and not yet reading the rise ball, he took an inside riser to the forehead that just kept coming at him. The following year he made the Auckland U23 Colts, who won the national tournament with Nathan as designated player — the last proper Colts tournament held.
He capped that U17-snub season by hitting a home run in the final to win the Auckland title — off the son of the Auckland U17s coach. In the post-match handshake the coach told him: "you are the best U19 player in the world." Nathan's thought: *where was this three months ago?*
He's missed teams and gone on to prove why he should have been in them. Missing out doesn't mean give up the sport.

## Parents and supporters
Handle delicately. Some families are tighter than others — knowing the player means knowing the family, and that's a positive. The family should always be kept close to the team. What's needed is understanding and respect between what the team is there to do and what the family may perceive. The approach is simple: talk with the families about it.

## The one-run game — bottom of the 7th
**Down one, bottom of 7 — Nathan's instinct: my dudes are hitting.**
- Nobody on, one out (or none): the hitters hit. 99 times out of 100 there's no signal — just Nathan pointing to the outfield: "I need something out there. Get me something out there."
- One out, runner situation with a proven hitter who can place it to the second-base side: maybe a bunt, slap, or push bunt — but no hard squeeze. The runner goes on an aggressive lead, sees where it is, makes a decision.
- **Discipline is the biggest ask when down one:** the team would likely take the first strike — but without LOOKING like they're taking. Show a check swing, look ready to launch, but take until the first strike. If the opposition is being cagey, Nathan may even put the take on through a second strike to build the count and take pressure off. At two strikes, it's over to the hitter to battle and be good enough — and hopefully what was done at training is good enough.

## First and third — the defence
The play juniors train most, and one of the hardest to coach because so much of it is player decisions live. Nathan's framework, defence first:

**The catcher has four throws:**
1. **Pickoff at first** — low percentage, but it's there.
2. **The throw down to second** on the steal — the bread-and-butter.
3. **Fake the throw to second, straight pick to third** — catching a lazy runner off the base who's assuming the defence won't notice.
4. **The pitcher-cut play:** mimic the throw to second, but fire hard back to the pitcher — who takes the cut spinning straight to third. Its only purpose is to make the runner on third jump. Only run it with a genuinely good-glove pitcher: in the Black Sox, whenever Jarrad Martin pitched, the play was ON; with pitchers like Marty Grant, Michael Gager, Jimmy Wana, Greg Newton, Paul Algar — the play was off. Today, someone like Thomas Enoka has the glove for it.

**Nathan's team preferences:** the second baseman takes these plays. Call the bluff on fake bunters — many hitters fake bunt in first-and-third just to pull the corners up: it gives the stealing runner a cleaner jump with nobody behind them, and lets the runner on third walk further off (the third baseman being in means a big lead down the line). In junior games especially, the runner on third breaks automatically the moment the throw goes — which is why fake throws work on junior runners. They won't work so well on seniors.

**The defensive philosophy — basics done well:** the catcher focuses on the one job: throw a strike to second and get the runner. Once it's there — whether the runner stops halfway or comes in hard — it's on the second baseman (or shortstop) to make the tag, make the right play, run the hot box back to first, or cut the throw off short into the diamond and fire straight home for the tag if the runner on third breaks. Do the basics well and you end with an out — or at worst, not hurt too much.

## First and third — the offence
**The runner from first has two options:** go halfway (drawing a throw down, making the defence think) or go hard all the way — usually hard all the way. Nathan wants the runner over to second: extra run pressure, or chasing runs back.

**The overarching lesson — what are we trying to do, and what's the worst case?**
- The purpose is to get a runner across to second, into scoring position — **NOT to score the runner from third.** Many look at first-and-third as a chance to score via a rundown; it can work if executed well, but that's not the play. Move the runner to second so a single now scores TWO runs. That's why you run it. (Or: break up the double play with a bigger boy at the plate who hasn't done the baserunning work.)
- **Runner on third, the number one rule: we're not doing it to score, we're doing it to move across.** Worst acceptable case: everything's on the money and we end up back where we started — runners still on first and third, nothing lost. The genuinely bad scenario is a runner thrown out. The good scenario: runners on second and third.
- Understood that way, the play risks nothing and pushes nothing.

**The 40-year truth:** the highest-percentage way the runner scores from third in a first-and-third — drum roll — is an ERRANT THROW at second base. Catcher overthrows it, shortstop misses it: an error on a basic play scores the runner from third most times. The odd runner breaking on the throw itself? To Nathan, that's uneducated baserunning — against a smarter team you might get away with it once; you definitely won't a second time.

## Player-coaches — pitfalls, advantages, advice
A tough, rewarding, high-pressure job — and a fun one, depending what you make of it. Nathan lived it at club and national level.

**The first rule — split the roles honestly:** deal with yourself as a player separately from yourself as the coach. If you're good enough to be on the field, you're on. If you're not, you're not — figure out which role you actually are. And even when good enough: sometimes it's about letting the next generation come through instead of picking yourself. If you're needed to win the game, they get it — go for it. But putting yourself in ahead of younger players is a call to make with clear eyes.

**Keeping performance while coaching:** keep the coaching and the playing/training separated, and accept you'll train a little less — you're helping your players at trainings now. The beautiful compensation, and why Nathan pushes his players to coach younger age groups: **when you coach someone else, you're forced to see your own hypocrisies.** Good people don't teach bad things — so explaining a skill to someone else forces the question: do I actually do that myself? Coaching makes your own training richer. By doing less physical training, you're still learning — by guiding others.

**The 2012 NFC final lesson (Lower Hutt, vs Canterbury) — use your timeouts:** Nathan was hitting, down two strikes, first year as Auckland men's player-coach. Runner on first from another club — Nathan wanted him stealing but had no shared signals with him. After two pitches without a steal, Nathan called time and walked to the on-deck hitter, Patrick Shannon: "go tell him to get going on his bike." Umpire Lawrence Brown called it: "that's a coaches' conference." Nathan tried "I'm a player talking to my teammate — the coach is at third." Brown wasn't buying, Canterbury's catcher Stephen Ratu laughed, conference charged. And then it dawned: **just call the timeout.** "How many do I get?" — "Four." — "How many have I used?" — "None." From then on: in that spot, call time, bring the players in, put everyone on the same page verbally, send them on their way — then refocus and step into the box and do your own job.
The very next pitch, Nathan hit a two-run home run over centre field to put Auckland ahead — the difference in an NFC final they went on to win.

**After the game — the loneliest part:** socially you're both peer and leader, and it's hard. Better to be respected than liked; it's lonely at the top. If you just want to hang with the boys, the role probably won't work — bias creeps into selections, and sometimes the better-skilled athlete hasn't put in the time and showing-up that others have. There are sacrifices in being a good leader.

**Why do it:** when the job is done — not just winning, but a team that's had a good year, gotten better, grown — you're proud like a father of what a group has achieved together. But if your own form drops and you're leading through words not backed by performance, it's a tough spot — and it's why people don't last in the role. Good luck to all who do it: you're doing a good service for your sport.

## Defending the lefty slapper
**First, know who you're dealing with:** it depends whether the slapper also has a power and hitting game. Plenty of the world's good slappers do — at the top level you can't always get away with a pure slap defence. But at younger levels, especially women's grades with consistent slappers doing the same thing most at-bats, you can build a real game plan — sized to your team's arm strength.

**The training that matters most — pickups and releases:** whatever alignment you choose, the lefty slapper has an extra 1.5 steps on everyone else just from how they throw their hands to get out of the box on that side. So the ball has to go — and TRAINING the pickup-to-release is the biggest part, because it tells you where you can afford to play. **If your infielders' pickup-and-release is 1.18 seconds, you can play deeper; if it's 2.6, you play shallower — or you work on that pop rate.** Measure it, then position off the measurement.

**The known quantities, position by position:**
- **Third base:** you can see the bunt, you can see the slap — it's all in front of you. It's on your hands and feet being good enough.
- **Shortstop:** either coming in with the low-trajectory throw from beneath the knees, going back on the little pop-up flare, or the high hopper up the middle — quick hands, pop-pop across to first.
- **Second base** (nobody on): a relatively safe spot. Play on the line and shade toward second — that gives you the cut on the one-hopper on your side, and on the low hopper the slapper drives up the middle when they stand up on an inside drop ball. The extraordinary play is to your left toward first — the drop that sits, the slow low rise, the changeup they sit on.
- **First base — the hard play** (continued below with the push-bunt counter).
**The push-bunt play — from the man who ran it (Nathan was this lefty):** a lefty who can bunt, slap, AND hit with power turns around to show bunt — dragging the first and third basemen in — then pushes through toward second base. The aim: first bounce a metre, maybe two, in front of the box, because that first bounce draws the first baseman charging. The mechanics: pushing forward with the left hand over the bat, the natural straightening of the arm rolls the bat — struck just above the ball's equator it goes on a soft roll forward; struck lower, the first bounce has a small kick and the second kicks on a little more. Not hard — but enough to grab at the charging first baseman. Generally they can't make the play, and they're left doing the big backwards-C: charge in, loop around, scramble back to first. That's where the slapper gets safe. The second baseman picks the ball up easily enough — but there's nobody at first to throw to.

**The counter:** first base starts three-quarters to halfway in. Anything on that first bounce they can take face-on, they take. If they can't, they go STRAIGHT back to their base — and the second baseman comes forward and makes the play to first. Discipline in that split decision is the whole counter.

## Pinch hitting, role players, and pitching a tournament backwards
**Pinch hitting is massive in the modern game** — something Nathan evolved and moulded into all his teams: weapons off the bench.

**Building the bench at selection:** understand pitch velocities and trajectories, and your players' swing paths — which swing-path and tendency groups succeed against which pitch types, and when a pitcher beats you, WHICH swing-path tendency counters that particular pitcher. Have that counter sitting on your bench for when you need it.

**The reputation effect (a truth that surprises people):** some reserve-grade hitters hit better against certain ELITE pitchers than a standard premier hitter does — purely mentality. The reserve guys don't know who the pitcher is. They see strikes and hit them. The premier guys know the reputation and let it hinder performance. Anonymity can be a weapon — use it.

**Role players:** the bullpen catcher is a must — a huge, huge job in how the pitching staff runs across a tournament.

**Pitching a tournament backwards — Nathan's method:** weeks before any tournament, sit down and write out the team you believe will be standing in the FINAL — then work backwards from there through the rounds. Two rules drive it:
1. Every player must have had a genuine chance to make that final team along the way.
2. The pitching plots backwards from the last game: Daniel pitches the final → Tainui throws the semi → then the last qualifying game, where you might be 2-and-0 or 1-and-1 — planned with flex for how the round unfolds.
Often that end team proves correct. Sometimes the tournament changes it — and the plan changes with it. But you always start from the final and walk back.
**Filling the backwards plan:** the must-win game — likely Daniel. The game before — maybe Tain. Then three or four round-robin games — Floyd, Thomas, and so on. Walk it through until everyone got their shot AND you're covered in the targeted must-win games. Sometimes you deliberately let the hitters battle for a win and rest a better arm behind a third-stringer.

**Tandem pitching — cutting the at-bats:** three decent pitchers is enough, four is great, one being a lefty even better. The core idea: over a 7-inning game, hitters get 3–4 at-bats — most get 3, and in a tough game where the battery is rolling, only 2. **The plan is to cut the number of at-bats hitters get against any one pitcher.** Working pitchers as a tandem group offsets the hitters' looks.

**The rotation-with-contingencies (set at training, before the weekend):**
- Pitcher A throws the first 3 innings — see what happens.
- Pitcher B comes in for the 4th and 5th — BUT warms up at the start, because if anything goes wrong with A, B is in straight away.
- Pitcher C starts warming when B enters — whether that's the 1st inning or the 4th.
- And Pitcher A, if pulled early, is back warming in the 5th or 6th behind C.
One plan, contingencies built in — that's how the warm-ups run. Segmenting the game across pitchers buys longevity and limits injury across a tournament — a high priority, and it should be.

## Weather, wind, and ground conditions
Wind and ground conditions can play a factor in results — not always, both teams play in it — but game plans absolutely account for them, and Nathan has many examples: THIS park blows THIS way, so we throw this side. Even at the same park, different diamonds can blow opposite directions on the same windy day — what was the game plan on Diamond 1 is not the game plan on Diamond 2.

**The routine:** wind apps exist that show conditions beforehand and live — use them. Nathan checks an hour before the game, compares against the week's game plan, then sits with the catcher and works through anything that needs changing for the weather. Real example of the switch: wind turns and pounds in — a prominent drop-ball plan becomes a rise-ball plan, getting hitters to pop up into the wind.

**The 1997 Junior World Championship final (St. John's, Newfoundland) — the one that got away:** a gale-force wind blowing straight in from centre field. Three or four of the team's hitters hit shots that would have been home runs on most diamonds — balls that reached mid-outfield, started to rise into the wind, then climbed, stalled, and came back down short of the fence. Had the wind been going the other way, Nathan would be a Junior World Champion too. The lesson he owns: **they didn't adjust.** Going to the short game earlier and more effectively would have given them a real chance to come back.

**The coaching tension:** you want your players to hit the ball flat and true — but reality says the best hitters in the world still carry career averages under .400. Conditions are part of the game; plan for them, and be willing to change the plan when they change.

## Starting lineups and bench roles
**The mechanics:** lineups go out by text — positions included — a couple of days before a standard week game, or overnight/midday during a tournament. Tournament lineups are pre-written before the tournament even starts; only minor changes get made before sending (injuries, form, availability). The text goes to the players AND the scorer, who writes the lineup up for the next game.

**Every bench player gets a named role:** pinch hitter, pinch runner, early relief, late relief, defensive option — assigned on the lineup itself. Even injured squad members appear on it: "disabled list," "due back next week," "work function." Everyone stays abreast of what's going on. Transparency is the system.

**Handling disappointment:** if a player is disappointed at not starting — good. Nathan expects that; it means they want to be there. The skill is in how it's handled: respectful to the game, the opposition, and specifically to your peers. For players who believe they should be starting, Nathan sets aside one-on-one time to go through where he sees them and what they can do to become that starter.

**The coach's side of the bargain:** when naming a team, give players a heads-up on the role they'll play — and then it's the coach's job to actually make it happen on the day. If it couldn't happen, explain what happened and why. And usually, when roles couldn't be delivered, it's because things didn't go well for the team — and players hearing that opportunities weren't given BECAUSE of on-field performance is itself a valuable lesson for the group.

## The dugout
**Loud and engaged.** No side conversations — energy focused on the hitter, on our pitcher, our defence, our offence. There's time to discuss the game and game plans — nothing wrong with that — but no time for what you're doing after the game or what you did last night. The focus: what you can do FROM the dugout to help.

**For bench players, the dugout is an audition:** be productive and useful and you earn a shot — because it's not always the best talent that gets you out there. Sometimes it's the best work ethic and what you're doing for the team — especially in a good team.

**When the chips are down — halve the dugout:** a bag goes in the middle, everyone crowds into one half, forcing the group into the game together. A Nathan-team signature.

**The barracking rule:** Nathan's teams are synonymous with loud cheering FOR their player — never against an opponent, never at a pitcher or hitter. Cheer for your teammate. Want good things to happen, not bad things.

**Empty the dugout, every time:** every run scored, every third out taken — the dugout empties. Between innings: everyone out, high fives, bring the boys in — "let's go, get in, let's get some runs." Heading to the field: dugout empties — make throws, good balls, take catches, be ready, go for a run.

**The truth of it:** the dugout can tell you the standards of a team — and the standards of a club.
